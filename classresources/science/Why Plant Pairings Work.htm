Why Plant Pairings Work

 

 

Large corporate farms generally have one solution to pests and weeds.  They use pesticides and herbicides.  Though the pesticides and herbicides they use are rated as safe by regulatory agencies, many activists disagree.  Two things are not in question:  1) pesticide, herbicide and fertilizer runoff from farms harm the health of water bodies.  2) Insects become resistant to these pesticides, so they become more difficult to battle.

 

Small farms have an advantage.  Because they are able to plant a variety of crops, and use plant pairs, they can use natural pest and weed control.  It takes more thought and planning to use natural pest and weed control, but that’s why you’re here; to think and plan.

 

Here are some ways that plants help one another grow better. 

 

Pest Control

 

·         Garlic: Repels Japanese beetle.  Japanese beetles feed on Strawberries, tomatoes, peppers, , blackberries, corn, peas and blueberries to name a few.

·         Celery:  Repels white moth (they eat cabbage, broccoli and other cabbage family crops.

·         Beans and Potatoes:  Beans repel Colorado potato beetles and potatoes repel Mexican bean beetles.  They should be planted in plots next to each other, not in the same plot

·         Onions family:  onions, leeks, chives, scallions, garlic and asparagus are stinky, so they repel lots of bugs. 

·         Marigolds:  Repel aphids, Japanese potato beetle and white flies

·         Hyssop, wormwood, thyme and sage:  These repel cabbage moth

·         Rue and white flowered geraniums:  Repel Japanese Beetles

·         Sage: Repels carrot flies and cabbage moths

·         Basil and Borage: Repel tomato hornworms

 

 

Weed Control

·         Lettuce, radishes, spinach and kale:  These fast growing crops can be grown close together to keep weeds out.  They can be grown with slower growing plants like tomatoes that will eventually grow above the low growing cover crops. 

 

Synergy:

·         In some case crops grown together grow better and taste better than when grown alone

 

Experiment

·         There is still a lot to learn about which crops grow best together.  Keeping your garden journal will help to learn more about which of our crops grow better together.
