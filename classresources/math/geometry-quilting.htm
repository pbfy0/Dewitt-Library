Geometry and Quilting

 

Quilts have been sewn for years.  They have played an important role in history. Some keep us warm or help bring color to a room.  During the time of the Underground Railroad, they were used as a means of communication and saved many lives.  Others are family heirlooms.  Geometry is an integral part of the quilting world.  You will see some examples of the geometry we have studied as you complete this project.  I hope you enjoy it!!!

 

There are a few good web sites I’d like you to peruse to become familiar with quilts, their history, color and patterns, and how GEOMETRY plays a big role in the art of quilting.  I don’t expect you to read every word.  Read as much as you would like while looking for patterns and color combinations.

Now you are ready to choose a pattern that is right for you.  There are a few things to keep in mind as you do this.

 

1.       Who is this quilted wall hanging intended for?  You or someone else?

2.     What colors and pattern do you think would be most appropriate for the person this piece is intended for?

 

 

 

Next, you must draw your pattern on a piece of graph paper.  Use a ruler!  Color each piece as close to the color of fabric as you can.  It helps when colors are very similar.  You can also assign numbers to the fabric and make a key.  You will use this as a guide when sewing your quilt.  Don’t lose it!!!

 

Now, measure and draw each of the pieces you need and cut them out of hard construction paper or cardboard.  You will use these as templates to cut your fabric.

 

Using the templates, cut out each piece of your fabric.   Be sure the fabric is face down.  That means the “right side” is not seen.

 

When you are ready to sew two pieces together, measure a ¼” seam from the edge of each piece and draw a light ¼” pencil line.  That will be your sewing line. You must do this on every piece that you sew or your pieces will not be even and lined up correctly.

 

As you progress with sewing pieces together, it is a good idea to have the seams pressed flat.  I will help you with that.  When you are finished piecing, you will be ready for batting and a backing.  The batting should be cut ¼” smaller than the quilted piece and the backing exactly the same size and the quilted piece.  The three pieces should be stacked like a sandwich.  You will sew the two sides and bottom only, and then turn the piece right side out, turn in the top seam, place tabs and finish sewing.
